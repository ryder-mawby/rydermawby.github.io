## Research

My work applies structural and reduced-form econometric methods to illicit markets and platform economies. The current focus is the geography of digital drug trade — how enforcement architecture, bilateral institutions, and platform anonymity shape where illegal transactions actually happen.

**Working paper: [Anti-Gravity Exists: Evidence from Darknet Drug Markets](https://github.com/ryder-mawby/darknet-gravity-model/raw/main/Mawby_Gravity.pdf)**

Using 353,983 listing-level observations from ten darknet cryptomarkets collapsed to a bilateral panel, I estimate PPML gravity models and find that standard gravity facilitation variables invert: contiguity, common language, and colonial ties all suppress bilateral drug trade — the opposite of every licit-goods result in the literature. Distance elasticity of −1.97 exceeds the canonical licit-goods range, consistent with an additional layer of enforcement deterrence unique to illicit trade. The suppression operates entirely through realized trade volume, not vendor advertising behavior, consistent with bilateral enforcement coordination raising interdiction risk on jurisdictionally familiar corridors.

A bilateral enforcement mechanism test shows routes with formal US mutual legal assistance treaty obligations generate substantially less conditional trade volume (−2.54***) — independent of the language coefficient — directly implicating institutional enforcement coordination rather than anonymity-seeking behavior as the operative mechanism.

The underlying data contribution is an 8-stage multilingual drug classification pipeline resolving vendor-entered listings into a 57-category pharmacological taxonomy across English, German, Dutch, Finnish, and French — publicly available and designed for use on any cryptomarket dataset.
